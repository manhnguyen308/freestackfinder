## Quick verdict

Most people do not need Notion or Obsidian to take notes — they need something fast, reliable, and free that works across their devices. **Google Keep** is the best quick-capture tool if you are in the Google ecosystem. **Apple Notes** is the correct answer for iPhone and Mac users who want zero friction. **Standard Notes** is the best choice for anyone who wants their notes encrypted and guaranteed to exist in ten years. **Simplenote** wins for minimalists who just want text, synced everywhere, for free. If you do need a workspace with databases and linked pages, see our [free Notion alternatives guide](/productivity/notion-alternatives/) — this article focuses on lighter-weight note capture.

## Why simple note-taking apps win for most people

The productivity software market has drifted toward complexity. Tools like Notion, Coda, and Obsidian are powerful — but they ask you to build a system before you can capture a thought. For most note-taking use cases (meeting notes, grocery lists, ideas, drafts, reading highlights), that overhead is a tax on the actual goal.

The apps that survive long-term note-taking habits tend to be the ones that open instantly, sync reliably, and get out of the way. A note-taking app that takes 30 seconds to open is one you will stop using within a month. The apps in this list are all genuinely free — not time-limited trials or "free up to X notes." They work.

## The best free note-taking apps in 2026

### 1. Google Keep — best for fast capture and Google ecosystem users

**What it is:** Google's note-taking app — a lightweight, colour-coded card interface built for quick capture, with reminders, labels, and seamless Google Docs integration.

**Free plan includes:**
- Unlimited notes
- Notes, lists, voice memos, and image notes
- Reminders and location-based alerts
- Labels and colour coding for organisation
- Collaborative notes — share and co-edit with others
- Syncs instantly across Android, iOS, and web
- Google Docs integration — export any note to a document in one click
- Completely free, no paid tier

**What the free plan is missing:**
- No markdown support — plain text and basic formatting only
- No nested notebooks or folder structure — labels only
- No end-to-end encryption
- No offline-first mode — requires internet for initial sync
- Notes cannot be very long — not suitable for document-length writing

**Who it's best for:** Anyone already using Gmail, Google Drive, or Google Calendar who needs fast, ubiquitous note capture. Students, professionals taking quick meeting notes, and anyone who frequently captures ideas on mobile and revisits them on desktop.

**Why it stands out:** Google Keep is genuinely frictionless. The widget on Android allows one-tap voice note or text entry without unlocking your phone. The reminders system is best-in-class for a free notes app — location-triggered reminders ("remind me about this when I get to the supermarket") are a feature most dedicated reminder apps charge for. The Docs integration is underrated: anything captured as a Keep note can be turned into a full document instantly.

[Try Google Keep →](https://keep.google.com)

### 2. Apple Notes — best for iPhone and Mac users

**What it is:** Apple's built-in note-taking app — deeply integrated with iOS and macOS, completely free, and significantly more capable than most users realise.

**Free plan includes:**
- Unlimited notes with iCloud sync (requires iCloud storage — 5GB free)
- Rich formatting: tables, checklists, headers, inline images
- Document scanning with the camera
- Handwriting recognition (search inside handwritten notes)
- Password-locked notes with Face ID / Touch ID
- Collaborate and share notes with other Apple users
- Smart folders (automated organisation by tag or date)
- Quick Note — capture from any app via Control Centre
- Completely free on Apple devices

**What the free plan is missing:**
- Apple ecosystem only — no native Android or Windows app (web access at iCloud.com is limited)
- Sync depends on iCloud free tier (5GB shared with photos and backups)
- No markdown support
- Not end-to-end encrypted by default (iCloud encryption applies, but not the same as zero-knowledge E2E)

**Who it's best for:** iPhone and Mac users who want the best-integrated, most capable free notes experience with no additional app to install or account to create.

**Why it stands out:** Apple Notes has been quietly adding power-user features for years. The document scanner with OCR is genuinely useful — scan a receipt or whiteboard and search for text within the image later. Smart folders automatically organise notes based on rules you set. For Apple users, there is no reason to pay for a third-party notes app — Apple Notes does more than almost any competitor.

*Available on iPhone, iPad, and Mac — free with your Apple ID.*

### 3. Standard Notes — best for encrypted, long-term note storage

**What it is:** An open-source, end-to-end encrypted note-taking app with a strong commitment to longevity and simplicity. The founders have explicitly committed to keeping Standard Notes free and operational indefinitely.

**Free plan includes:**
- Unlimited notes and tags
- End-to-end encryption — Standard Notes cannot read your notes, even if they wanted to
- Sync across unlimited devices
- Plain text and basic markdown on free tier
- Available on Windows, macOS, Linux, iOS, Android, and web
- No ads, no data collection
- Open-source — auditable code

**What the free plan is missing:**
- Rich text editor, spreadsheets, and advanced themes require a paid plan
- No image attachments on free tier
- No collaboration features

**Who it's best for:** Privacy-focused users, journalists, researchers, or anyone who wants to be certain their notes are unreadable to third parties. Also ideal for users who have been burned by note apps shutting down (Evernote's decline, Simplenote's ownership changes) and want maximum longevity guarantees.

**Why it stands out:** Standard Notes' differentiator is trust and permanence. The encryption is zero-knowledge — the company cannot decrypt your notes even under a legal request. The longevity pledge is published on their website: the app is designed to work even if the company ceases operations, because the format is open. For notes you need to keep for years — legal documents, journal entries, research — Standard Notes is the most defensible free option.

[Try Standard Notes →](https://standardnotes.com)

---

### 4. Simplenote — best minimalist markdown notes across all platforms

**What it is:** A bare-bones markdown notes app from Automattic (the company behind WordPress.com) — text only, synced instantly across every platform, entirely free.

**Free plan includes:**
- Unlimited notes
- Markdown support with live preview
- Instant sync across all platforms
- Version history — recover any previous version of a note
- Note sharing via link
- Tags for organisation
- Available on Windows, macOS, Linux, iOS, Android, and web
- Completely free — no paid tier exists

**What the free plan is missing:**
- Text only — no images, attachments, or rich formatting beyond markdown
- No end-to-end encryption
- No reminders or due dates
- No nested tags or folder structure
- Collaboration is read-only via shared link — no co-editing

**Who it's best for:** Writers, developers, and minimalists who want a plain markdown scratchpad synced instantly across every device. The cross-platform support (Linux included) is rare for a free notes app.

**Why it stands out:** Simplenote is the only app on this list that is completely free with no paid tier — Automattic runs it as a public good and open-source project. The version history is genuinely useful: every change is saved, and you can roll back to any point in a note's history. For someone who writes in markdown and just wants their notes accessible everywhere without friction or fees, nothing beats it.

[Try Simplenote →](https://simplenote.com)

### 5. Notion free tier — most powerful free tier, but with a learning curve

**What it is:** The free tier of Notion — a workspace tool that includes unlimited pages, databases, and AI-light features, but requires more setup than a dedicated notes app.

**Free plan includes:**
- Unlimited pages and blocks
- Basic database views (table, board, list, calendar, gallery)
- Templates
- Collaborative editing with up to 10 guests
- 5MB file upload limit
- Available on web, Windows, macOS, iOS, and Android

**What the free plan is missing:**
- Version history limited to 7 days
- Advanced AI features require paid
- No offline mode
- Page history and audit log require paid
- Complex setup compared to dedicated note apps

**Who it's best for:** Users who need more than notes — linked databases, project tracking, wikis, and structured content all in one place. Not the right tool if you just want to capture ideas quickly.

**Why it stands out:** Notion's free tier is genuinely powerful for structured knowledge management. If you are building a personal wiki, a reading log, or tracking projects alongside your notes, Notion does things no other free app can match. For quick capture and simple note-taking, however, the overhead is unjustified — use Google Keep for that, and Notion for structure.

## Decision guide — which app should you use?

**Use Google Keep if:** You are on Android or Chrome, capture ideas on the go, and want the fastest possible path from thought to saved note.

**Use Apple Notes if:** You are primarily on iPhone and Mac and want the most capable free notes experience with zero extra setup.

**Use Standard Notes if:** Privacy and longevity matter more than polish — you want to know your notes are encrypted and will be accessible in 10 years.

**Use Simplenote if:** You write in markdown, use Linux, and want a truly free, no-upsell experience with version history.

**Use Notion if:** You need databases and linked pages alongside your notes, and are willing to invest time in setup.
