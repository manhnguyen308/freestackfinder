## Quick verdict

**Google Calendar** is the strongest free calendar app for most people — unlimited events, cross-platform sync, and deep integration with Gmail and Google Meet at zero cost. **Notion Calendar** (formerly Cron) is the best choice for professionals who want a fast, keyboard-driven scheduling experience with Notion integration. **Apple Calendar** is the obvious pick for anyone in the Apple ecosystem — it works seamlessly across iPhone, iPad, Mac, and iCloud at no cost. **Proton Calendar** is the right choice if privacy matters and you want end-to-end encrypted events. **Zoho Calendar** rounds out the list for small teams already using Zoho's free productivity tools. ## Why the right free calendar app matters

Most people default to whatever calendar came with their phone or email account. That works for basic use, but it often means missing features that save real time: smart scheduling suggestions, cross-service event detection, better keyboard shortcuts, or stronger privacy defaults.

The free calendar market in 2026 is strong. Google Calendar remains the default, but Notion Calendar has emerged as a serious option for professionals, and Proton Calendar has made privacy-first scheduling accessible. The right choice depends on your ecosystem — Apple, Google, or Microsoft — and whether you need a personal planner, a work scheduling tool, or something that coordinates across a small team.

The main trade-offs: Google Calendar has the deepest integrations but requires a Google account and ties your schedule data to Google. Apple Calendar is tightly integrated on Apple devices but less capable cross-platform. Proton Calendar offers genuine privacy but has fewer integrations. Notion Calendar is fast and elegant but requires a Notion account and is less suitable for shared team calendars. Zoho Calendar is the most complete team option at no cost but requires a Zoho account.

## The best free calendar apps in 2026

### 1. Google Calendar

**What it is:** Google's full-featured calendar, available free with any Google account — the most widely used calendar app in the world.

**Free plan includes:**
- Unlimited events, calendars, and recurring events
- Gmail integration — automatically adds events from booking confirmations, flight receipts, and reservations
- Google Meet integration for creating video call links directly in events
- Shared calendar functionality for coordinating with others
- Works on iOS, Android, and all major browsers
- Appointment scheduling pages for free (limited slots)

**What the free plan is missing:**
- Google Workspace (paid) adds admin controls, custom domains, and organisational sharing
- Some appointment booking features are more limited on the free tier versus Workspace plans
- No end-to-end encryption for event content

**Who it's best for:** Anyone who uses Gmail or Google Workspace and wants a calendar that works across every device and platform without setup friction.

**Why it stands out:** Google Calendar's main strength is passive intelligence — it reads your Gmail to automatically add events you would otherwise have to enter manually. Flight bookings, restaurant reservations, package deliveries, and event tickets appear in your calendar without any action from you. For heavy email users, this alone saves meaningful time each week. The cross-device sync is also effectively instant, and the web interface is fast enough to use as a daily driver without installing an app. For team scheduling, shared calendars and the "find a time" feature for group events work well at no cost. Pair it with a [free note-taking app](/productivity/free-note-taking-apps/) to keep meeting notes alongside each event.

[Use Google Calendar free →](https://calendar.google.com)

### 2. Notion Calendar

**What it is:** A standalone calendar app from Notion, built on the foundation of the Cron calendar app (acquired by Notion in 2022) — free for anyone with a Notion account.

**Free plan includes:**
- Full calendar functionality with day, week, and month views
- Google Calendar sync — displays your existing Google Calendar events
- Notion integration — link calendar events directly to Notion pages, tasks, and databases
- Keyboard-first design with shortcuts for creating and editing events quickly
- Zoom and Google Meet integration for video call links
- Available on Mac, iOS, Windows, and Android

**What the free plan is missing:**
- Requires a Notion account — not standalone without Notion
- Team calendar features are more developed on Notion's paid plans
- Microsoft Outlook sync is limited compared to Google Calendar integration

**Who it's best for:** Professionals who already use Notion for notes, tasks, or project management and want their calendar to connect directly to that work — so a planning session links to a Notion doc, and a project deadline appears in the calendar.

**Why it stands out:** Notion Calendar solves a specific problem: the gap between your schedule and your work. Most calendars hold events but have no connection to the documents, tasks, or projects those events relate to. Notion Calendar closes that gap — you can link a calendar event to the Notion page you plan to work on, so your Tuesday afternoon block for "quarterly report" links directly to the draft. The keyboard-first interface is also genuinely faster for creating and editing events than most calendar apps. If Notion is already the centre of your planning workflow, this is the most natural calendar choice. For a deeper look at Notion's capabilities, see our [best free Notion alternatives guide](/productivity/notion-alternatives/) — the comparison includes Notion's own free tier.

[Use Notion Calendar free →](https://calendar.notion.so)

### 3. Apple Calendar

**What it is:** Apple's built-in calendar app, available on every iPhone, iPad, Mac, and Apple Watch at no cost — syncs through iCloud.

**Free plan includes:**
- Unlimited events across multiple calendars
- iCloud sync across all Apple devices — instant
- Siri integration for creating events with voice
- Shared iCloud calendars for family or team coordination
- Native integration with Apple Maps, Contacts, and Mail
- CalDAV support for connecting to Google Calendar or other external calendars
- Available on Mac, iPhone, iPad, and Apple Watch

**What the free plan is missing:**
- Limited to the Apple ecosystem for native sync — cross-platform experience on Windows and Android is weak
- Fewer smart features compared to Google Calendar (no automatic event detection from email)
- No web app equivalent as capable as Google Calendar's browser experience

**Who it's best for:** iPhone or Mac users who want a calendar that works seamlessly across Apple devices without any configuration — and who do not need to coordinate regularly with non-Apple users.

**Why it stands out:** Apple Calendar's advantage is zero friction for Apple users. It is already installed, already synced via iCloud, already connected to Siri and Maps, and works on every Apple device you own without creating a new account or installing anything. For personal scheduling on Apple devices, nothing requires less setup and works more reliably. The iCloud shared calendar feature also covers basic family or small group coordination — you can share a calendar with a partner, family member, or housemate and see their events in your view in real time.

[Use Apple Calendar free →](https://www.icloud.com/calendar)

### 4. Proton Calendar

**What it is:** A privacy-focused calendar from Proton (the company behind ProtonMail), with end-to-end encryption applied to all event data.

**Free plan includes:**
- End-to-end encrypted events — Proton cannot read your calendar data
- Proton Mail integration for a fully encrypted email + calendar combination
- One shared calendar on the free plan
- Mobile apps for iOS and Android
- Web interface at calendar.proton.me
- Import support for .ics files from other calendar apps

**What the free plan is missing:**
- Free plan limits you to one shared calendar (paid plans add more)
- No native integration with Google Calendar or Apple Calendar
- Fewer third-party integrations compared to Google Calendar
- Desktop apps for Mac and Windows are in development or limited compared to the web app

**Who it's best for:** Users who want genuine privacy for their schedule — journalists, people in sensitive roles, or anyone who prefers that their calendar data not be readable by a platform provider.

**Why it stands out:** Proton Calendar is the only mainstream calendar app where the event details are encrypted end-to-end — meaning Proton itself cannot read what your events say. This matters for anyone whose schedule contains sensitive meeting subjects, confidential client names, or personal information they prefer to keep private. Google Calendar and Apple Calendar both process and can read your event data as part of how they deliver features like automatic event detection. If privacy is a priority and you are already using ProtonMail, Proton Calendar is the natural pairing and the most capable private scheduling option available for free.

[Use Proton Calendar free →](https://calendar.proton.me)

### 5. Zoho Calendar

**What it is:** A calendar tool within the Zoho productivity suite, free with any Zoho account — designed for individuals and small teams.

**Free plan includes:**
- Multiple calendars with colour-coding
- Event invitations and attendee management
- Integration with Zoho Mail, Zoho CRM, and other Zoho apps
- Team calendar view for coordinating across a small group
- Recurring events and reminders
- Available on web, iOS, and Android

**What the free plan is missing:**
- Best value when used with other Zoho tools — less compelling as a standalone calendar
- Fewer integrations with non-Zoho tools compared to Google Calendar
- The interface is more functional than polished

**Who it's best for:** Small teams or freelancers already using Zoho Mail or Zoho CRM who want a calendar that connects naturally to the rest of their Zoho workflow without adding another tool.

**Why it stands out:** Zoho Calendar's advantage is ecosystem fit for Zoho users. If your email lives in Zoho Mail and your contacts are in Zoho CRM, having a calendar that connects directly to both — so a CRM contact can be added to a meeting in one step — saves the switching friction you would have coordinating between Zoho and Google Calendar. The free tier is genuinely functional for small team scheduling, and Zoho's suite is broad enough that many small businesses run on it without paying. For teams also using [free communication tools](/productivity/slack-alternatives/), Zoho Calendar integrates natively with Zoho Cliq (Zoho's messaging tool).

[Use Zoho Calendar free →](https://www.zoho.com/calendar/)

## When to pay for a calendar app

The free options above handle almost every personal and small-team scheduling need. Paid calendar upgrades make sense in specific situations: if you need advanced appointment booking pages with payment collection, if you run a service business where clients self-schedule from your calendar, or if you need admin-level controls over a company-wide shared calendar.

Fantastical's paid plan ($4.99/month) is worth considering if you are an Apple user who wants a fast natural-language event creation experience and deeper task integration. Calendly's paid tier makes sense once you need more event types or team-level booking pages. For most individual users and small teams, the free options above are sufficient without a subscription.
